Running Stories

Florence Griffith Joyner

Incredible Resilience

Griffith began running at the age of 7 years old, and kept running in college at the University of California.

Griffith ran in the 1980 U.S. Olympic Trials, and later won the silver medal in the 1984 Los Angeles Olympic Games in the 200 meter race. After this race, she stepped back from the sport, disappointed with her performance.

In 1987, three years later, she re-entered the sport of sprinting with passion. In 1988, she smashed the previous Olympic record by 0.27 seconds in the 100 meter sprint, setting the new record at an astonishing 10.49 seconds. Later that year, she won 3 gold medals and 1 silver medal at the 1988 Seoul Olympics.

Source: Encyclopaedia Britannica – Florence Griffith Joyner

Usain Bolt

Overcoming Adversity

Conventional wisdom was that sprinters needed to be short, yet standing at 6 feet and 5 inches, Usain Bolt was nowhere near short. Short runners were understood to accelerate faster out of the blocks (the angled platforms runners use to push off at the start of the race) due to a lower center of gravity and faster stride turnover. While Usain Bolt was slower out of the blocks, once the sprint phase ended he was able to make up the distance with his incredible stride.

Usain Bolt was born on August 21, 1986, in Trelawny Parish, Jamaica. At age 16, he set the junior 200 meter world record in 20.13 seconds. He later failed to advance beyond the 200-meter heats at the 2004 Olympics in Athens due to a hamstring injury. In 2007, he returned and earned a silver medal at the 2007 World Championships. In May 2008, he ran 9.76 seconds, an incredible time, and later that year he ran 9.72 seconds in the 100 meters.

He continued to improve, and at the World Championships in August 2009, he ran 9.58 seconds in the 100 meter race and 19.19 seconds in the 200 meter race. Both of these records continue to hold.

Source: Encyclopaedia Britannica – Usain Bolt

Michael Johnson

The Golden Spikes

Michael Johnson was born in 1967 in Dallas, Texas. He was a notable sprinter in the high school 200 meter race, and started running at Baylor University in 1986. In 1989, he set the indoor 200 meter U.S. record.

In 1992, he won an Olympic medal and set a world record at the 1992 Barcelona Games in the 4 x 400 meter relay. In the 1996 Atlanta Games, he decimated the 200 meter world record by 0.34 seconds, setting the new record at 19.32.

During this incredible 200 meter race in 1996, he wore bright golden running spikes which drew attention. He later donated these iconic shoes to the World Athletics Heritage Collection.

Sources: Encyclopaedia Britannica – Michael Johnson
